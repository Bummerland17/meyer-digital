# 🛡️ Anti-Scam Verification System
## Real Estate Wholesaling — Wolfgang Operations

> **Rule Zero:** If it feels wrong, it is wrong. Walk away. No deal is worth being defrauded.

---

## SECTION 1: SELLER VERIFICATION CHECKLIST

> **Policy:** Every seller must pass ALL checks before you pursue a contract. No exceptions.

### Step 1 — Ownership Verification

**Before the first site visit or detailed conversation:**

- [ ] Get the full property address (street, city, state, zip)
- [ ] Look up the owner of record at the county assessor/appraiser website (see Section 4)
- [ ] Confirm the name on the listing/lead **exactly matches** the owner of record
- [ ] If names differ — ask for written explanation before proceeding
  - Acceptable: recent death (estate), recent divorce, recent purchase (deed not yet recorded)
  - Unacceptable: "I'm just handling it for a friend," vague relationship, refusal to explain
- [ ] Screenshot and save the county record for your deal file

**Free Tools:**
- County assessor website (see Section 4 for all 10 states)
- `netr.com` — nationwide property record search (free basic search)
- `publicrecords.netronline.com` — free county record directory by state

---

### Step 2 — Title Search Basics (Free)

**Check these before writing any offer:**

- [ ] Go to the county recorder/clerk website (same as assessor, often same portal)
- [ ] Search deed history — look for the chain of title
  - How long has seller owned it?
  - Were there recent rapid transfers? (Possible fraud indicator)
- [ ] Look for any recorded liens, judgments, or lis pendens (notice of lawsuit)
- [ ] Search seller's full name in the same portal for judgments
- [ ] Check for HOA liens if applicable (call HOA directly — free)
- [ ] Note: Free title search is preliminary only. **Always use a title company at closing** — this is non-negotiable.

**Specific Free Lien/Judgment Resources:**
- Federal tax liens: `https://efts.irs.gov/EFTS/` — free IRS tax lien search
- Bankruptcies: `https://pacer.uscourts.gov` — federal bankruptcy search (small per-page fee but very cheap)
- State court judgments: search county clerk court records (same portal as deeds)
- UCC filings (business liens): state Secretary of State website

---

### Step 3 — Occupancy Status

**At the property, before or during appointment:**

- [ ] Drive by the property before calling the seller back — check for signs of occupancy (cars, lights, mail, children's toys, active utilities)
- [ ] If occupied: confirm who lives there
  - Is it the seller themselves?
  - Is there a tenant? (Verify lease terms, expiration, rent amount)
  - Are there squatters? (Different legal process — verify county eviction rules)
- [ ] Ask directly and clearly: "Is anyone living at the property right now? Tenants or anyone else?"
- [ ] If tenants: request a copy of the lease before signing any contract
- [ ] Lease transfer rules vary by state — confirm with your title company
- [ ] **Never sign a contract claiming vacant if you suspect occupancy**

---

### Step 4 — Lien & Encumbrance Check

- [ ] County recorder: check for mortgage(s), home equity lines, mechanics liens
- [ ] Search city/municipality for code enforcement violations and water/sewer liens (call city directly — free)
- [ ] Check for IRS liens (see above)
- [ ] HOA: call HOA directly, ask for payoff letter and outstanding dues
- [ ] Verify property taxes are current: county tax collector website (same portal or separate)
  - Most counties show tax delinquency publicly for free
- [ ] Note total estimated encumbrances vs. your offer price — ensure there's room to close clean

---

### Step 5 — Identity Verification of Seller

**For in-person meeting:**

- [ ] Request government-issued photo ID (driver's license or passport)
- [ ] Confirm name on ID matches name on county deed exactly
- [ ] Photograph or copy the ID (with permission — standard industry practice)
- [ ] If seller refuses to show ID: **do not proceed**

**For remote/phone seller:**

- [ ] Request a selfie holding their ID — non-negotiable
- [ ] Video call preferred over phone-only for remote deals
- [ ] Search seller's name + address on social media to verify real person
- [ ] If seller is an estate/trust/LLC:
  - Executor: request Letters Testamentary from probate court (public record)
  - Trust: request trust certificate or pour-over documents
  - LLC: verify LLC ownership at state Secretary of State website (free)
  - Corporate officer: request Articles of Incorporation + corporate resolution authorizing sale

---

### Step 6 — Double Contract Check

- [ ] Ask directly: "Have you signed any other purchase agreements or given anyone else an option on this property?"
- [ ] Include representation warranty in your PSA: seller warrants no other contracts exist
- [ ] Title company will catch most of these at closing — but catching it early saves your time
- [ ] If seller seems evasive about other buyers: walk away or insist on seeing any prior contracts

## SECTION 2: BUYER VERIFICATION CHECKLIST

> **Policy:** Never share deal details (address, price, potential profit) until buyer passes verification.

### Step 1 — Initial Screening Questions

Before sharing anything:

- [ ] "Are you a direct cash buyer or do you use hard money/financing?" (Cash preferred; financing adds risk of deal falling through)
- [ ] "What markets do you actively buy in and what price range?"
- [ ] "How many properties have you purchased in the last 12 months?"
- [ ] "Are you looking for fix-and-flip, buy-and-hold, or both?"
- [ ] "Who handles your closings?" (They should name a title company or attorney readily)
- [ ] Responses should be immediate and specific — vague answers = red flag

---

### Step 2 — Proof of Funds (POF) Requirements

**What real POF looks like:**
- Bank statement, brokerage account, or line of credit statement
- Current (dated within 30 days)
- Shows the buyer's name or entity name matching their business card/introduction
- Sufficient liquid funds to cover the purchase price (not just 10% down)
- Account number partially visible (not fully redacted — full redaction is suspicious)
- Realistic bank name (Chase, Wells Fargo, Bank of America, local credit union — not obscure foreign banks)

**What fake POF looks like:**
- PDF with inconsistent fonts or formatting
- Balance that's suspiciously round (exactly $500,000.00 with no cents)
- Date/header shows PDF metadata from different software than the stated bank
- Bank letterhead that doesn't match any real bank's branding
- No account number visible at all
- Statement shows no transaction history — just a balance (real statements have activity)

**How to verify POF:**
- [ ] Call the bank directly (look up the number independently — NOT from the letterhead) and ask to verify the account balance
- [ ] Banks can confirm "sufficient funds" without disclosing exact amount — just ask "can you confirm this account has $X available?"
- [ ] Cross-reference with Google: search "[Buyer Name] real estate investor [City]" — legitimate buyers have a digital footprint
- [ ] Request a letter from their bank on official letterhead if statement seems off

---

### Step 3 — Track Record Verification

- [ ] Ask for 3 recent deals they closed — get: address, purchase price, close date, title company used
- [ ] Verify closed sales at the county recorder (same free portals from Section 4)
  - Search their name or entity — deeds should show up as grantee
- [ ] If they claim to own rentals: check county tax records — ownership should show their name/entity
- [ ] Call 1-2 title companies they've worked with (ask: "Has [buyer name] closed deals with you in the last year?")
- [ ] If they can't name a single verifiable closed deal: **do not proceed**

---

### Step 4 — Reference Check Process

- [ ] Request 2-3 references from sellers or other wholesalers they've bought from
- [ ] Call the references (do not text — you want a real voice conversation)
- [ ] Ask references: "Would you sell to them again? Were there any issues at closing? Did they close on time?"
- [ ] Search their entity name on Google + "complaint" or "scam" or "lawsuit"
- [ ] Check BBB: `https://www.bbb.org`
- [ ] Search PACER for lawsuits involving their entity name

---

### Step 5 — Escrow-Only EMD Policy

**Non-negotiable rules:**

- [ ] ALL earnest money deposits go directly to the title company escrow — NEVER to you, never to the buyer's attorney, never to the buyer
- [ ] Wire instructions must come from the title company directly — in writing, on official letterhead, confirmed by phone
- [ ] Never change wire instructions based on an email request — ALWAYS call the title company to verify changes
- [ ] If a buyer suggests sending EMD anywhere other than the title company: **immediate disqualification**
- [ ] EMD amount should be meaningful: minimum $500 for deals under $50K, minimum 1% for larger deals
- [ ] EMD receipt from title company required before sharing full deal package

---

### Step 6 — Assignment Fee Protection

- [ ] Assignment agreement must be signed BEFORE the buyer receives closing docs or deed info
- [ ] Assignment fee must be payable to YOU directly at closing via separate disbursement
- [ ] Title company must have a copy of your assignment agreement and be instructed to pay your fee at closing
- [ ] Never allow buyer to interact directly with seller without you present or informed
- [ ] Do NOT record assignment agreement before closing (keeps your fee private from seller)
- [ ] Include non-circumvention clause: buyer cannot contact seller directly to renegotiate or cut you out

## SECTION 3: CONTRACT PROTECTION RULES

### The Never-Do List (10 Ironclad Rules)

1. **Never sign a contract as principal on a property you haven't personally verified ownership on.** Period.

2. **Never accept EMD into your personal or business bank account.** All EMD to title company escrow only.

3. **Never share a deal address, full details, or ARV until the buyer has passed verification AND signed an NDA or buyer agreement.**

4. **Never allow a closing to proceed without a title company or real estate attorney handling the transaction.**

5. **Never accept verbal agreements.** Everything in writing. No exceptions.

6. **Never give a seller any money (EMD, option payment) before you've verified their ownership.**

7. **Never let a buyer or seller rush you.** Artificial urgency ("I need this done by tomorrow or I'm selling to someone else") is a manipulation tactic.

8. **Never take a deal where the seller wants to be paid in cash, Bitcoin, wire to foreign account, or any non-standard payment method.**

9. **Never sign over power of attorney to anyone involved in the transaction.**

10. **Never proceed if the title company discovers discrepancies and the seller can't explain them with documentation.**

---

### Required Clauses in Every Purchase Agreement

Include ALL of the following in your PSA:

```
1. TITLE CONTINGENCY: Purchase is contingent upon delivery of clear, insurable title.

2. OWNERSHIP WARRANTY: Seller warrants they are the sole owner of record and have full legal 
   authority to sell the property. Seller has not entered into any other purchase agreements, 
   option agreements, or rights of first refusal on the property.

3. OCCUPANCY DISCLOSURE: Seller discloses all current occupants (tenants, family members, 
   squatters). Seller is responsible for delivering property vacant (or with tenants, as agreed 
   in writing) at closing.

4. DISCLOSURE OF ENCUMBRANCES: Seller discloses all known liens, judgments, mortgages, 
   HOA obligations, code violations, and encumbrances affecting the property.

5. ASSIGNMENT CLAUSE: Buyer (or its assigns) — this explicitly allows assignment.

6. INSPECTION PERIOD: Buyer has [X] days from execution to conduct due diligence and 
   may cancel for any reason during this period.

7. EARNEST MONEY: EMD held in escrow by [Title Company Name]. Not released to seller 
   without buyer's written consent except upon closing or buyer default.

8. CLOSING DATE: Closing to occur on or before [DATE]. Time is of the essence.

9. DEFAULT PROVISION: In the event of seller default, buyer shall be entitled to 
   specific performance or return of EMD plus liquidated damages.

10. IDENTITY VERIFICATION: Seller has provided valid government-issued photo ID, 
    which has been verified and retained by buyer.
```

---

### Assignment Agreement Must-Haves

Your assignment agreement (between you and the end buyer) must include:

```
1. IDENTIFICATION OF ORIGINAL CONTRACT: Full description of the original PSA 
   (parties, property, price, date).

2. ASSIGNMENT FEE: Exact dollar amount of your fee, payable at closing via 
   separate wire or check from title company.

3. ASSUMPTION OF OBLIGATIONS: Assignee (buyer) assumes all obligations of the 
   original contract from the date of assignment.

4. NON-CIRCUMVENTION: Assignee shall not contact the original seller directly 
   to renegotiate, modify, or enter into any new agreement without Assignor's 
   written consent. Violation is subject to [damages].

5. NO FURTHER ASSIGNMENT: Assignee may not further assign this contract without 
   Assignor's written consent.

6. TITLE COMPANY INSTRUCTION: Parties agree that the Title Company shall disburse 
   the assignment fee to Assignor at closing from Assignee's closing funds.

7. EARNEST MONEY: Assignee's EMD of $[amount] shall be deposited to [Title Company] 
   within [X] business days of signing this agreement.

8. CLOSING DEADLINE: Assignment must close on or before [original PSA closing date].

9. REPRESENTATIONS: Assignee represents they have sufficient funds to close without 
   financing contingency (if applicable).

10. GOVERNING LAW: [State] law governs this agreement.
```

---

### Earnest Money Handling Policy

**Standard Operating Procedure:**

1. **You as buyer from seller:** Your EMD goes to title company escrow — this protects you from the seller claiming they never received it, and protects the seller from you absconding with their deal.

2. **Your buyer's EMD from you:** Must come to the title company — same reasons. Never goes to you personally.

3. **Wire instructions:** Always get wire instructions directly from the title company by phone before wiring anything. Call the number from their official website — NOT from the email containing the wire instructions.

4. **Change of wire instructions:** If anyone emails you "updated" wire instructions, this is a wire fraud red flag. Call the title company immediately.

5. **EMD release:** EMD is only released at closing or by mutual written consent. Title company should not release to seller unless deal closes or you've signed a cancellation.

## SECTION 5: RED FLAG DETECTOR — 20 SCAM SIGNALS

> If you encounter ANY of these, stop, verify, or walk away. Multiple red flags = automatic walk.

### Conversation Red Flags

1. **"I need to close in 3 days — it's urgent."**
   Artificial urgency is the #1 manipulation tactic. Legitimate sellers can wait 2-3 weeks. If they can't, something is wrong.

2. **"I can't meet in person — I'm overseas / in the military / caring for sick relative."**
   Classic setup for remote fraud. You cannot complete a proper deal without in-person or video ID verification.

3. **"Just send the deposit directly to me — I'll hold it until closing."**
   This IS the scam. There is no legitimate reason EMD goes to a seller. Full stop.

4. **"My brother/cousin/friend owns the property — they gave me permission to sell it."**
   Permission isn't legal authority. Require formal documentation (POA, executor letters). No exceptions.

5. **"I'll send you proof of ownership later — let's just get the contract signed first."**
   Proof of ownership comes FIRST. Always.

6. **"Don't worry about the liens — they'll get paid off before closing."**
   Undisclosed liens that "will be paid off" are a title nightmare. Verify now, not later.

7. **"I know what I'm doing, I've done this many times — you don't need all that paperwork."**
   Experienced sellers know paperwork protects everyone. Resistance to documentation = red flag.

8. **"Can you wire the earnest money to this account?" (unexpected mid-deal change)**
   This is wire fraud. Stop. Call the title company from their official website number.

### Listing/Ad Red Flags

9. **Price is 40%+ below market with no explanation.**
   Could be legitimate distress, but verify everything 2x harder. Too good to be true usually is.

10. **Listing has no photos or only stock/generic photos.**
    Ghost listing indicator. Drive by before doing anything.

11. **Listing uses copy-pasted description from another market or has mismatched details.**
    Classic fake listing. Cross-reference address on Google Maps Street View.

12. **Multiple listings for the same property with different prices/sellers.**
    Search the address on Zillow, Redfin, and Craigslist simultaneously. Double listings = chaos.

13. **"Owner financed — no qualifying."** on a Craigslist ad from a private person with no verifiable identity.
    Common scam entry point. Verify ownership before any conversation.

### Buyer Red Flags

14. **"I'll take everything you send me — I buy any deal in any market."**
    Real buyers have criteria. Buyers who want everything are time-wasters or scammers.

15. **Proof of funds is a PDF with no transaction history — just a balance line.**
    Real bank statements show deposits, withdrawals, and activity. A statement with only a balance is fake.

16. **Buyer insists on meeting at a coffee shop to exchange assignment documents with cash.**
    All transactions go through title companies. Cash document exchanges are how assignments get stolen.

17. **"Let's just do this between ourselves — we don't need a title company."**
    A buyer who wants to avoid a title company is planning to either steal your fee or defraud the chain of title.

### Process/Deal Red Flags

18. **Seller becomes unreachable after you send your offer or sign a contract.**
    Could be backing out, could be running a parallel scheme. Stay in contact or cancel.

19. **Title company can't reach the seller for closing — seller "will be wiring a signature."**
    Sellers sign in person or via legitimate notary. Remote signing with unverifiable identity is fraud.

20. **Anyone pressures you to skip a step "just this once."**
    Every fraud starts with "just this once." The process exists for a reason. Never shortcut it.

## SECTION 6: SAFE PLATFORM USAGE GUIDE

### Craigslist

**What to Trust vs. Verify:**
- TRUST: Nothing until verified. Craigslist is anonymous by design.
- VERIFY: Every single lead independently via county records before engaging.

**Common Scam Patterns on Craigslist:**
- **Ghost listings:** Property was sold/rented months ago; scammer reposts to collect info/money from eager buyers
- **Duplicate rental-to-sale scam:** Same property listed as rental AND for sale by different people — verify ownership for both
- **Photo theft:** Scammers use photos from real listings for properties they don't own
- **Email harvesting:** "Respond to this ad for details" — they collect your info, send phishing attempts, never sell a real property
- **No-contact insistence:** "Email only, no phone calls" — legitimate sellers will talk to you

**Safe Engagement Rules:**
1. Always reverse image search any property photos (Google Images or TinEye) before calling
2. Verify ownership at county assessor BEFORE calling the number in the ad
3. Never wire money or share banking info based on a Craigslist contact alone
4. Meet only in daylight at the property — never at an office you can't verify
5. Use a Google Voice or separate number for initial Craigslist contacts
6. If they won't video call or meet in person: move on

---

### Facebook (Groups, Marketplace, Ads)

**What to Trust vs. Verify:**
- TRUST: Facebook accounts that have years of history, real photos, verifiable local activity
- VERIFY: Every deal regardless of how legitimate the profile looks

**Common Scam Patterns on Facebook:**
- **Fake investor profiles:** New accounts (< 1 year), few friends, generic photos, claiming to be cash buyers
- **Account impersonation:** Scammer creates nearly identical profile to a real investor (e.g., "John Smith RE Investor" vs. "John Smyth RE Investor") to steal deals or money
- **Lead gen traps:** "We buy houses" ads that collect seller leads and sell them — the "buyer" never intends to close
- **Group pump-and-dump:** Scammer posts fake success stories in Facebook wholesaling groups to build credibility, then solicits deals/deposits
- **Marketplace ghost listings:** Same as Craigslist — posts for properties they don't own

**Safe Engagement Rules:**
1. Before sharing any deal with a Facebook contact: check their profile creation date, friend count, and posting history
2. Reverse search their profile photo using `images.google.com` — scammers use stock photos
3. Search their name + "real estate investor" + their city on Google — real investors have a footprint
4. Video call before sharing any deal details — impersonators won't video call
5. Verify their entity name at the Secretary of State website before signing anything
6. If someone claims to be a known investor: contact that investor through their official website or prior verified number — not through Facebook messages

---

### BiggerPockets

**What to Trust vs. Verify:**
- TRUST: Profile age, post history, deal count listed on profile, forum reputation
- VERIFY: Closed deals, references, and POF for any actual transaction

**Common Scam Patterns on BiggerPockets:**
- BP is lower-risk than Craigslist/Facebook, but:
- **Fake track records:** Members can list whatever they want on their profile — verify deals via county records
- **Newbie impersonating experienced investor:** Reads the forums, talks the talk, but has never closed
- **Forum solicitation bait:** Posts like "looking for wholesalers in [market]" to collect your deal flow, then disappears or doesn't close
- **Mentorship/coaching scams:** Not directly deal-related, but common — someone offers "JV deals" in exchange for upfront coaching fees

**Safe Engagement Rules:**
1. For any buyer found on BP: still require POF, track record check, and escrow-only EMD — BP membership is not a substitute for verification
2. Check their post history and forum reputation — experienced investors have years of substantive posts
3. Use BP to research markets and network, but treat any transaction the same as any other source

## APPENDIX: QUICK CONTACTS & RESOURCES

### Title Companies (establish relationships in each market)
- Find reputable title companies: search "title company + [city]" on Google Maps, look for companies with 4+ stars and 50+ reviews
- Ask for referrals from local REIA (Real Estate Investors Association) groups
- Use the same title company repeatedly to build a relationship — they'll flag issues faster for known clients

### Free Legal Help
- Many state bar associations have free legal hotline hours for real estate questions
- NOLO.com — free plain-English legal guides on real estate contracts by state

### Report Fraud
- FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (wire fraud): `ic3.gov`
- FTC Consumer complaints: `reportfraud.ftc.gov`
- State real estate commission: report fraudulent agents/sellers
- Local law enforcement: file a police report immediately if defrauded — required for insurance claims
