# Founder TikTok/Reel Scripts — Wolfgang Meyer
*Written by Reel. Reviewed by Hawk.*

## Script 1: "The Morning Routine"

**Hook (0–3s)**
> "I don't check Instagram when I wake up. I check what my agents did."

**Body (3–45s)**
> Pull up Telegram. Scout found three leads overnight — companies in the UK that match the profile I gave him. Quill already drafted the outreach emails. Alex queued the follow-ups.
>
> I haven't touched a keyboard yet. My coffee's still hot.
>
> I'm one person running multiple products out of Namibia. I don't have a team in an office. I have a system — seven AI agents, each with a job, each running while I sleep.
>
> Most people think you need to hire before you can scale. I haven't hired anyone. I just got the system right.

**End (45–55s)**
> If you're curious what that system actually looks like — I'll show you. Follow along.

---

**Visual Notes:**
- Open on hands holding phone, morning light
- Cut to Telegram screen — notifications from Scout, Quill, Alex (real names visible)
- Show message previews: "3 leads found," "Emails drafted," "Calls queued"
- Cut to face — calm, not surprised. This is just normal.
- End on workspace, coffee, quiet

**Tone Notes:**
Unhurried. Matter-of-fact. No music swell. The point lands because it's delivered flat, like you're just describing your morning.

---

### 🦅 Hawk Review — Script 1

**Human voice:** Strong. "My coffee's still hot" is the kind of specific, throwaway detail that makes it feel lived-in. Not performed.

**No AI slop:** Clean. No "revolutionize," no "game-changer," no "unlock your potential." Every sentence is functional.

**Accuracy:** Agent names (Scout, Quill, Alex) are real. The workflow described is plausible and honest — no overclaiming. ✅

**Brand voice:** This is Wolfgang. Quiet. Direct. Lets the system speak instead of hyping it. The "I haven't hired anyone" line does real work.

**Score: 9/10**
*Minor note: "Got the system right" could be slightly more specific — what does "right" mean? But for a 45-second hook reel, it earns its ambiguity.*

## Script 2: "I Built This From Namibia"

**Hook (0–3s)**
> "Everyone assumes I'm in San Francisco. I'm in Namibia."

**Body (3–45s)**
> Southern Africa. Not a tech hub. Not a startup ecosystem. No VC firms down the road, no co-working spaces full of founders comparing pitch decks.
>
> Just me, a solid internet connection, and whatever I can build.
>
> PantryMate is live. UnitFix is live. SmartBook AI is live. All of it built from here — from a desk in a country most people can't point to on a map.
>
> I used to think location mattered. I thought you had to be somewhere to be taken seriously. Turns out the internet doesn't ask where you're from. It just asks what you ship.
>
> I ship things.

**End (45–55s)**
> Veldt. Building from the veldt, for anywhere.

---

**Visual Notes:**
- Open with a slow pan of the Namibian landscape or surroundings — wide, quiet, distinctly African
- Cut to desk, screens, products loaded in browser tabs (pantrymate.net, unitfix.app visible)
- Show the actual live products — not mockups
- End frame: Wolfgang at desk, skyline or nature visible behind him

**Tone Notes:**
No chip on the shoulder. No "proving something." Just stating facts. The pride is in the work, not the narrative.

---

### 🦅 Hawk Review — Script 2

**Human voice:** "A country most people can't point to on a map" — that's honest and slightly self-deprecating in a way that feels real, not performative. Good.

**No AI slop:** Passed. "The internet doesn't ask where you're from, it just asks what you ship" walks the line between punchy and slogany — but it earns it because the concrete list before it grounds it.

**Accuracy:** Product names and URLs are real and confirmed live. "Veldt" brand name is accurate. ✅

**Brand voice:** The ending — "Veldt. Building from the veldt, for anywhere" — is the right kind of quiet. Not a rallying cry. A statement.

**Score: 8.5/10**
*The "I used to think location mattered" pivot risks sounding like a TED Talk beat. Consider trimming to make it faster. But overall, this one will land hard for anyone building outside a tech city.*

## Script 3: "My AI Called 72 Businesses While I Slept"

**Hook (0–3s)**
> "Seventy-two businesses got a phone call from my company last night. I was in bed."

**Body (3–50s)**
> This is Vapi. It's an AI voice platform. I built a product on top of it called SmartBook AI — it handles outbound sales calls for businesses that don't have a sales team.
>
> I set the agent up, gave it a script, pointed it at a list. While I was asleep, it worked through 72 calls. Real conversations. Qualifying leads. Booking appointments.
>
> The businesses that didn't pick up got a follow-up queued automatically.
>
> I woke up to a summary.
>
> This isn't experimental. It works. And other businesses can have this exact system running for them — that's the whole point of SmartBook AI. You don't need to hire a sales rep to do outbound. You need an agent that doesn't clock off.

**End (50–60s)**
> SmartBook AI is available now. Link's in the bio if you want to see what it looks like.

---

**Visual Notes:**
- Open on phone on nightstand — dark room, sleeping
- Cut to Vapi dashboard showing call queue — 72 entries, statuses, timestamps
- Show agent config screen — voice, script, parameters
- Show call log with completed/scheduled status
- End: morning light, Wolfgang reviewing summary on laptop

**Tone Notes:**
The number leads. Don't soften it. "I was in bed" lands because it's stated plainly. Show the actual dashboard — not a demo, the real thing.

---

### 🦅 Hawk Review — Script 3

**Human voice:** "I woke up to a summary" is the best line in this script. Four words. No decoration. That's the whole story.

**No AI slop:** Solid. "Doesn't clock off" is a little well-worn but it's specific enough in context that it doesn't feel hollow.

**Accuracy:** Vapi is real. SmartBook AI is a real product. The 72-call claim needs to be verified before filming — if that's a real number from a real night, use it. If not, replace with an actual number. **Do not approximate.** ✅ (assuming the number is real)

**Brand voice:** Direct and functional. The explanation of SmartBook AI is clear without being salesy. "That's the whole point" is Wolfgang's register — blunt, honest.

**Score: 9/10**
*The ending CTA ("link's in the bio") is the weakest line — a little flat. But it's honest, and for this type of content, that's fine. Could be cut entirely and the video still converts.*

## Script 4: "I Have $60 of Food in My Fridge and I Still Ordered Delivery"

**Hook (0–3s)**
> "I had sixty dollars of food in my fridge and I still ordered delivery. That's the problem PantryMate solves."

**Body (3–40s)**
> Not because I was lazy. Because I opened the fridge, saw a half block of cheese, some leftover rice, eggs, a pepper — and had no idea what to make.
>
> So I just... ordered something instead.
>
> PantryMate is stupid simple. You scan what you have. Or you type it. In about thirty seconds it gives you three meal options using exactly what's in your kitchen.
>
> That's it. That's the app.
>
> No subscriptions. No meal kits. No "premium AI chef experience." Just — here's what you can make right now.
>
> I built it because I kept doing the exact thing I just described. Turns out a lot of people do.

**End (40–50s)**
> pantrymate.net — free to try.

---

**Visual Notes:**
- Open on a real fridge — not staged perfectly. Real food, slightly chaotic
- Show the scan/input flow in PantryMate
- Show the three meal suggestions appearing on screen
- Keep the UI demo fast — under 15 seconds
- End on the fridge again, then the suggestions. Simple loop.

**Tone Notes:**
Slightly self-deprecating. The founder is the user. That's the whole authenticity engine here — he built it for himself first.

---

### 🦅 Hawk Review — Script 4

**Human voice:** This is the most human script of the five. "A half block of cheese, some leftover rice, eggs, a pepper" — that specificity is everything. It's exactly what people see in their own fridge. Nobody says "I had random ingredients." They say what the ingredients actually were.

**No AI slop:** The explicit "No premium AI chef experience" line is a clean self-aware joke that defuses any hype before it forms. Good instinct.

**Accuracy:** pantrymate.net is live. The 30-second claim needs to reflect real app performance — if it takes longer, adjust. ✅

**Brand voice:** This is the most approachable script — which is right, because PantryMate is a consumer app, not an enterprise product. The tone shift from Scripts 1-3 is appropriate and smart.

**Score: 9.5/10**
*Best script in the set. The hook is a genuine hook — it's a behavior most people recognize immediately. The ending is clean. "That's the app" is perfect.*

## Script 5: "I'm Building an App Factory"

**Hook (0–3s)**
> "I'm not building an app. I'm building a machine that builds apps."

**Body (3–50s)**
> The idea is this: most solo founders build one thing, grind on it, hope it works.
>
> I'm doing something different. I have a process. Scout — one of my AI agents — does the research. Is this problem real? Is there a market? What's already out there? He comes back with a report.
>
> If it checks out, I build. Usually with Lovable or Cursor — I can have something shippable in a week, sometimes less.
>
> Then it goes to Product Hunt. AppSumo. Real users, real feedback, fast.
>
> If it sticks — I keep building. If it doesn't — I learned something cheap and I move on.
>
> PantryMate came out of this. UnitFix came out of this. More are coming.
>
> One person. A pack of AI agents. No funding. No team. Just a tighter loop than most studios run.
>
> That's Veldt.

**End (50–60s)**
> I'll keep showing you how it works.

---

**Visual Notes:**
- Open on a simple flowchart or whiteboard sketch: Research → Build → Launch → Decide
- Cut to Scout's research output on screen
- Cut to Lovable/Cursor in action (real build, not staged)
- Quick cuts: Product Hunt listing, AppSumo page
- End: Wolfgang at desk, products visible, calm

**Tone Notes:**
This is the meta-script — the one that explains everything else. Deliver it steadily. Not breathless. This is someone describing a system they've already built, not pitching one they're imagining.

---

### 🦅 Hawk Review — Script 5

**Human voice:** "I learned something cheap and I move on" — that's the line that makes this real. It acknowledges failure without dramatizing it. Very human. Very Wolfgang.

**No AI slop:** The opening hook flirts with being clever but earns it because the body immediately gets specific. "Machine that builds apps" could read as hype — but "Scout comes back with a report" grounds it fast.

**Accuracy:** Lovable, Cursor, Product Hunt, AppSumo — all real tools in the stack. PantryMate and UnitFix confirmed live. "No funding, no team" — accurate per the brief. ✅

**Brand voice:** "That's Veldt." — two words to end it. That's the right register. No flourish. Just the name.

**Score: 8.5/10**
*The weakest moment is "tighter loop than most studios run" — slightly competitive/comparative in a way that doesn't fit the rest of the tone. Could cut that sentence entirely and it's stronger. Otherwise this is a solid capstone script that sets up a whole content series.*

## Hawk's Overall Notes

Five scripts. No slop. Here's the ranking:

| # | Script | Score |
|---|--------|-------|
| 4 | Fridge / PantryMate | 9.5/10 |
| 1 | Morning Routine | 9/10 |
| 3 | 72 Calls | 9/10 |
| 2 | Built from Namibia | 8.5/10 |
| 5 | App Factory | 8.5/10 |

**What works across all five:**
- Specific numbers and names ground every script
- No script tells the viewer what to think — they show and let it land
- The tone is consistent: Wolfgang talks like someone who's already done the thing, not someone who's about to

**One global note:**
Film in one session if possible. The visual continuity — same desk, same surroundings, same quiet — tells its own story. The consistency is the brand.
