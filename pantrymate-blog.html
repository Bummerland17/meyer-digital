Pantry Cooking Guide

What Can I Cook With What I Have?
(The Honest Answer)

How to turn random fridge and pantry ingredients into an actual meal — without a grocery run.

You open the fridge, stare for thirty seconds, and close it again. Then repeat. It's one of the most universal experiences in home cooking — you have food, but nothing looks like a meal.

Here's the honest answer: the ingredients you have right now are almost certainly enough to make something good. The problem isn't your fridge. It's the mental overhead of pattern-matching random ingredients to a dish on the fly.

Let's fix that — with a simple framework first, and the right tools second.

The Formula That Works Every Time

Good meals almost always follow one of these formats:

  1. Stir-fry — protein + vegetable + aromatics (garlic, ginger, onion) + soy sauce or any savory sauce over rice or noodles
  2. Frittata / egg dish — eggs + whatever vegetables or cheese you have, cooked in a pan or oven
  3. Soup or stew — nearly anything simmered in broth with onion and garlic becomes dinner
  4. Grain bowl — rice, quinoa, or any grain + protein + roasted or sautéed vegetables + a drizzle of sauce
  5. Pasta improvised — pasta + whatever's in your fridge + olive oil, garlic, and parmesan if you have it

Start by identifying what you have in each of three categories: protein (eggs, canned beans, frozen chicken, tofu), starch (pasta, rice, bread, potatoes), and vegetable or aromatics. If you have at least one from each, you have dinner.

How to Actually Stop Wasting Food

The average household throws out roughly 30% of the food it buys. That's not laziness — it's a system problem. Ingredients get pushed to the back of the fridge, forgotten, and eventually tossed.

The fix is simpler than it sounds: cook in order of expiry. Before you plan anything, scan your fridge for what's closest to going bad — wilting herbs, half-used cream, a leftover roasted vegetable. Build tonight's meal around those first.

Quick rule: Anything that'll be bad in 2 days gets used tonight. Everything else gets used this week. Only then do you check what you're missing and shop for it.

Keeping a live pantry list — even a basic one on paper — helps enormously. You stop buying duplicates of things you already have, and you start cooking from stock instead of shopping for every individual recipe.

Practical Tips to Use Right Now

  • Do a 5-minute fridge audit tonight. Group what you see by protein / starch / vegetable. Pick one from each and name the dish. You'll be surprised how quickly it clicks.
  • Keep a "use first" shelf at eye level in your fridge. Anything that's about to expire lives there — guaranteed to be seen every time you open the door.
  • Mise en place your pantry once. Spend 20 minutes pulling everything out of your cupboard, grouping by category, and tossing anything expired. You'll cook from it more often once you can see it clearly.
  • Stock five flavor-makers. Garlic, soy sauce, olive oil, canned tomatoes, and some kind of vinegar. These turn nearly any combination of proteins and vegetables into something worth eating.
  • Plan backwards from your expiring ingredients, not forwards from a recipe. Ask "what needs to be used?" first, then "what can I make with this?"
